History of
Vinyl Records

Vinyl’s story began in the early 1900s, from fragile shellac discs to the long-playing records introduced in 1948. Through the 1950s to the 1980s, vinyl defined how people listened to music. Thanks to collectors, artists, and fans, it’s made a powerful comeback, proving that great sound and classic style never go out of fashion.

Types of Vinyl Records

Vinyl records come in a few different formats, each with its own charm and purpose. LPs (long plays) are the most common and typically hold full albums, offering that immersive start-to-finish listening experience. EPs, or extended plays, sit between singles and LPs—perfect for shorter collections or special releases. Singles usually feature one track per side and were once the go-to way to share hit songs. Collectors and music lovers enjoy all three for their unique sound quality, artwork, and the tactile joy of playing a physical record.

Essential Equipment

The complexity or ease of your setup depends on how minimalist or tradtional you want to go. For now, we will go with a traditional setup. That means at minimum you will need the following:

  • Turntable: The base unit that spins the vinyl record.

  • Preamp: An electronic device that amplifies the signal from the cartridge to a level suitable for further amplification by an external amplifier or receiver. Some turntables come with a preamp built-in, be sure to check if your model requires one.

  • Amplifier/Receiver: Boosts the audio signal to drive speakers and produce sound.

  • Passive Speakers: Convert the amplified electrical signals into audible sound waves.

You can swap out components like the amplifier or speakers as your preference changes. Using passive speakers allows for more flexibility in upgrading individual parts of your audio setup over time.
