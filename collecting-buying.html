Buying Used Vinyl Records:
A Guide for Beginners

Where to Find Records and What to Look For

Who buys used vinyl records? Collectors, music enthusiasts, and anyone looking to explore the rich world of analog sound. Buying used vinyl can be a rewarding experience, offering access to rare finds, classic albums, and unique editions that may not be available in new releases. Whether you're a seasoned collector or just starting out, understanding where to find quality used vinyl and what to look for can enhance your collection and listening experience.

What to Look for When Buying Records

The first thing to know about how to collect records is that you will heavily rely on your eyes. When you are looking at a record, you want to check the condition of both the vinyl and the sleeve. The vinyl should be free of scratches, warps, and other damage that could affect sound quality. The sleeve should also be in good condition, with no tears or creases. You should also check the label on the record to make sure it is the correct one for the album you are looking for. Finally, you should check the price of the record to make sure it is within your budget.

At some point, you will run into the issue of bootleg records. The first sign is that they are often priced lower than the market value. Quality of bootlegs can vary widely, so it's important to inspect them closely. Look for inconsistencies in the label design, unusual packaging, or poor-quality vinyl. If you're unsure, consider consulting with a knowledgeable collector or using online resources to verify authenticity.

Tips Before You Buy

Vinyl Identification

For those venturing into the world of buying vinyl, its more than snagging a good deal; its about uncovering pieces of music history! Here is aguide to help navigate through your vinyl hunting experience.

  • Condition is Key Look for scratches, scuffs, and warping, as these affect sound quality. A record in "mint" condition will paly without any surface noise, while "very good" indicates minor surface noise. Avoid records lableled as "poor" or "fair."

  • Check the Vinyl Color Some vinyl releases are marbled or colored, adding to their appeal to collectors. Color can affect sound quality but the record will still hold its value. These colored vinyls are often irreplicable meaning the mixed colors are unique for each pressing.

  • Inspect the Cover The album cover is part of the package so make sure it is in decent condition. Look for splits, ring wear, and water damage. A record with an intact cover will hold more value.

  • Pay Attention to Pressings Different pressings of an album can vary in sound quality and value. Some collectors prefer early pressings, which may have unique mixes or masterings. Research the album ahead of time so you can determine which pressing suits your preferences.

  • Rating System Many record stores and sellers use a standardized grading system to describe the condition of their vinyl:

    • Mint (M): Perfect or nearly perfect condition, often unplayed.
    • Near Mint (NM or M-): Almost perfect, with no visibles signs of wear.
    • Very Good Plus (VG+): Slightly used with minor signs or wear.
    • Very Good (VG): Some surface noise or scratches, but plays well.
    • Good: Noticeable scratches or scuffs, may affect sound quality.
    • Poor (P )/ Fair (F): Significant wear, may have skips or distortion.
